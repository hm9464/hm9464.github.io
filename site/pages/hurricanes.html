Atlantic Hurricane Analysis

Jupter notebooks for all data loading, parsing, and analysis can be found here

Background

Hurricanes have become increasingly prominent in recent years due to a combination of factors; most notably, climate change. According to weather.gov, prime conditions for a hurricane to form include:

  • Sea temperature of at least 26 degrees Celsius (80 degrees Farenheit)
  • Latitiude of at least 5 degrees N/S of the equator (cannot form at the intertropical convergence zone due to the Corioles force that causes hurricanes to spin).
  • High relative humidity.

This project will analyze hurricane paths in recent seasons, where storms tend to be at their strongest, including visual analysis and statistical trends over time.

Data Source

HURDAT2 Data from NOAA

The NOAA records all historical hurricanes and tropical storms in their hurdat dataset. For this project, we will focus on the Atlantic hurricane seasons from 1850-2017. The data are structured with recordings every 6 hours (0000, 0600, 1200, 1800). Hence, one limitation of this dataset is that it does not consider PEAK intensity when determingin strength of a storm, everything is relative to time.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Before diving into the analysis, lets take a look at the data at a high level to understand the attributes and features of the dataset better. We will look at the number of hurricanes in each year, and the overall number of classified tropical storms in each year. A hurricane is a storm categorized as having wind speed > 74mph.



There seems to be a definite trend towards an increasing number of storms since 1851. What's interesting to note is that there are more years with > 10 hurricanes compared to in the past. In the period 1851-1990, there were 9 years where the number of hurricanes was 10 or greater. Since 1990, there have been 6 years with 10 or more hurricanes.

Let's take a look at the storm paths over time. We will compare the paths for two 10-year time periods to visualize how the number and strength of storms has changed over the years. The darker the path is, the stronger the storm. Hover over the path for the storm name, and click any part of the line to get information on what the storm's status was at the time (including wind speed, category, minimum pressure, date and time).

From the above maps, it is evident that there have been significantly more hurricanes in the last 10 years compared to the 1908-1917 period. It's also interesting to note the number of storms at higher latitudes in the 2008-2017 period, which indicates that warming sea tempeatures may be allowing tropical storms to enjoy a wider range of latitudes, and could potentially be contributing to the higher number of overall storms being observed.

Now, lets look at the average duration of tropical storms to see if tropical storms have been increasing in length over time. According to the HURDAT2 data dictionary, data on minor tropical storms and depressions were only started in the 1950s and 1960s. If we include all data from 1851, this may skew the results as data from 1850-1960s may miss out minor tropical storms. Therefore, we will only look at data from 1970-2017.



It seems like there isn't much of a difference in terms of average storm duration over the course of the last 100 years. I have yet to determine the cause for the spike in the 1870s, although it could be put down to a lack of accurate data.

Finally, lets look at the number of landfalls over our time period. We would expect there to be more tropical storms and hurricanes making landfalls as there have been more storms in general as time has gone on.



This plot provides a good illustration that our assumption is correct. In the late 1800s and early 1900s, it was very rare for tropical storms to make landfall, whereas now it seems to be very common. The frequency of multi-year landfalls has also risen sharply in the last two decades. Note: Data is missing for the period 1960-1980.
